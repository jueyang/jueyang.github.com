The protagonist is a technologist hic et nunc (portfolio, resume, artist rooms). She once wanted to become an architect and might have achieved that philosophically.

She has had a few loose narratives -- the.train, very first play, poems, ongoing words -- which she believes should be harnessed into the discipline of

Playwright

I am a technologist...Yes, that's a term. It's vague, and whenever I use the word people follow up by asking, "so what does that mean." Usually I just simplify it and say I teach journalists how to code.

God

I thought simplification bugged you. You are proud of the liberal arts way of defining things.

Playwright

Yes, I call it the fluffy way. But nuanced discussions are not always immediately available. Many conversations are 10 seconds long and dead.

God

You think you are being labeled and so you don't bother to explain.

Playwright

Exactly.

God

Ain't a good attitude.

Playwright

(takes in the words)

You are right. My defense mechanism in a world filled with pre-judgements, impatience, and small talks is contributing to the very problem.

(beat)

You know what's funny though? When I wanted to be an architect a few years ago, I was told -- by my mentors, by the books, by the idols I had at the time -- to LIVE, and do so with tremedous humility.

(beat)

An architect is the very profession of empathy.

(finds it profound, yet funny)

Now I have empathized with computers.

God

And that's a metaphor for?

Playwright

The fact that I am more rational and efficient and, to some extent, desensitized. I've become very pragmatic and refuse to call myself an artist.

God

Well first of all, what you described is called growth... Second, what's wrong with calling yourself an artist?

Playwright

You make things for yourself. And that's selfish.

God

An artist can be as selfless or narcissistic as she wants to be.

(beat)

And you know you have idealized the profession of an architect, right?

Playwright

Yes. Yes... But that was the period where I...

(pulls out a sketchbook from the bookshelf and flips to a page)

I had this epiphany once. I was at an architecture critique with all the guests. One of my studio mates was presenting, but I was so deep in thought with my own project. My brain was filled with words and images and sketches... but all of a sudden -- beyond the control of my consciousness -- they started to zoom out, like a camera that was very close started to pull away, away...and away...until all those letters and drawings became a square. One. Single. Square. Dense with information, yet unintelligible. The camera moved on to a different block of words and images, close up, and the same thing happened -- away...away...and away!

(points to a place on the page)

I ended up with all these neatly organized squares. It was the definition of patterns. Patterns! I remember thinking, is this what abstraction is? Is this how my brain organizes chaos? Is it, actually, how the human mind works???

(more calmly)

The first time I organized a bunch of code into objects I had a flashback of just that. Later I learned more vocabularies in programming: patterns, in the programming sense, modules... I have finally found the precise words to describe that moment of epiphany.
God

Now that's what you should say when people ask you what a technologist means.

Playwright

Ha. If I could get them to listen for more than 10 seconds, I guess.

(beat)

But yeah, it is deeply satifying to see the connections between different disciplines -- and to be able to get very good at something you thought you never would. I love metaphors. Data structure, building structure. Binding data to a node, associating meaning with a name. It's all very philosophical. It's almost as if you were learning a new language AND doing stand-up comedy in it, and people laugh because your wit is real despite your mother tongue. Of course I've endured the boring, the stressful, the demanding -- times of production. On those days it feels like working on a construction drawing of a condo that you didn't design.

(unrolls tracing paper of old drawings)

Ironic, isn't it. The production process across all fields.

(beat)

And I've been blocked, many many times, by concepts and new knowledge that were too much to swallow. I've made a fool of myself by blowing the scope -- that's today's speak of "you fucked up the budget". I took on more than what I could chew. But I learn. I learn in the end.

God

So you have lived with humility, to some degree at least. You have learned valuable lessons, practicality being one of them, and you ar...

(interrupted by the movements of playwright, who has begun to sweep the floor)

What are you doing?

Playwright continues, and gathers a pile of artifacts, including: a cardboard architecture model, the unrolled tracing paper, a few colorful maps, a keyboard, a sleek monitor.
God

(picks up the monitor and uses it as a mirror)

Huh. My left side is better-looking.

(finds the incredulous look on the playwright's face)

What?

Playwright

(regarding the pile)

I think I have a new pattern here.

God

Is that why you invited me? To check out your new pattern?

Playwright

Yes.
